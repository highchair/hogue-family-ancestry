[Pierre Hogue]({% link _people/1648-pierre-hogue.md %}) leaves France from the port city of St. Malo, on a ship called the “St. Michel”, and arrives in “New France” in the summer of 1672 along with recruits of the Carrignan Regiment. The regiment is there to help quell hostilities with the native tribes and provide protection for the settlers. It is unclear if he is a member of this group, but some sources have him rising to the rank of sergeant in the <span lang="fr">Levasseur</span> Company of the regiment. Upon completion of his enlistment, he remains in “New France”, marries, and begins a family. His service provides him with a monetary award of 100 <span lang="fr">livres</span> (a French currency unit possibly equal to the English pound) and a piece of land of 4 <span lang="fr">arpents</span> (roughly 3 and half acres).

Following is additional information I received from Hiral Hogue, a long distant cousin living in Canada, which comes from some historical publication (source unknown to me) and is as follows (which I translated from French as best I could:

It reads:

“Pierre Hogue, son of [Jean Hogue]({% link _people/1625-jean-hogue.md %}) and Nicole Dubus, from the parish of Bellfontaine, near Amiens in Picardie, enters into a marriage contract with [Marie-Madeleine Canachita]({% link _people/1654-marie-m-nachita.md %}), a continental native (<span lang="fr">Amerindienne</span>), on Monday, 14 November 1672. They are wed on Tuesday the 22nd in Montreal. Their marriage produces two children before she dies 3 years later on Monday, 28 September 1676. Their first born [Claude]({% link _people/1673-claude-hogue.md %}) dies at the age of 13, and [Pierre]({% link _people/1675-pierre-hogue.md %}) dies at the age of 22, a drowning victim (circumstances unknown).

“Pierre remarries in Montreal on Tuesday, 10 November 1676, to [Jeanne Theodore]({% link _people/1663-jeanne-theodore.md %}). She is baptized in Montreal on Wednesday, 8 August 1663, daughter of Michel Theodore and Jaqueline Lagrange. She is 13 years of age at this point in time, but the marriage is probably not consummated until 1679 and her first child is born in January of 1680. This union (of Pierre and Jeanne) produces 8 children.

“Upon her father’s death and the subsequent sale of a parcel of land, Jeanne receives 100 <span lang="fr">livres</span>, and she and Pierre settle in Riviere-des-Prairies in 1681, where he tries his hand at trapping and fur trading. However, he is not successful, and continually runs into debt. Jeanne, fearing that Pierre will ruin them both, obtains a separation of property in December of 1698. Due to another inheritance from the father’s estate, she purchases land in Riviere-des-Prairies in 1700, eventually exchanges that for another piece of land (possibly smaller and of less value) in Isle Jesus (present day Laval) in 1707, with the difference in value to be paid to Jeanne by an annual rental of 31 livres and 19 sols, which she in turn uses to pay off Pierre’s debts. They relocate to Isle Jesus in 1712 on land given to Pierre from “le Seminaire de Quebec” (circumstances unknown), and after a while they return to Riviere-des-Prairies where he dies on 1725 at the age of 77.”

The circumstances of this relationship are unknown. She and Pierre presumably reconcile because they have two additional children after her “affair”, one in 1703, and the last child in 1705. She dies in <span lang="fr">Riviere-des-Prairies</span> in 1730 at the age of 67.”

***

As stated earlier, the above is my translation from a document I received from Ms. Marie-Josee Hogue, which she received from her father Hiral Hogue. Cleary, Pierre and Jeanne had their problem, which, in the course of my research, seems to be the rule rather than the exception for the Hogue clan.

The first 300 years of existence for“the Hogue’s must have been difficult, and I guess you could say, still continues today in some respects. [Albert]({% link _people/1880-albert-j-n-hogue.md %}), generation #9, my “Pepere Hogue”, was a millworker, and apparently doing well, until the Great Depression hit New England, and he lost everything. My father, [Damase]({% link _people/1923-damase-a-hogue.md %}), also worked in the mills, joined the Navy during WWII, married, and played the trombone in a local band to provide some additional income for a growing household. My mother, as well, also worked in the mills for a time, in addition to bringing up a family and the responsibility that goes along with raising children. With nothing better than a 8th grade education, I suppose mill work was the best option for them both until, eventually, all the mills went south, where labor and operating costs were lower. My Mother and Father found other jobs, but life was always a struggle.

And life for our forefathers could not have been any easier. Unfortunately, the information contained in my research tell of only of the birth, marriage, and death of these individuals; a very small part of life (albeit very important ones). I have no information on their day to day lives, and I can hardly imagine what it was like. And I wonder if I could have done any better under the circumstances. Hopefully, “The Hogue Family Tree” and its contents provide some insight of who we are, and where we come from.
